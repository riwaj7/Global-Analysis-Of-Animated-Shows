Brenda Kong
Riwaj Khadka
Alex Labenz

This project will take a detailed look at the growth and development of animated television over the past 70 to 80 years. With the rapid increase in popularity of television and animated shows over the past couple of decades, it is only natural that the production of these shows would evolve over time. This kind of evolution includes many different factors and not necessarily ones strictly directly linked to the series, like the animation technique used or how many years it aired. Other elements will be explored as well, including the influx in the number of shows produced by a specific country overtime or which channels dominated with the most shows aired during certain timeframes.

Conclusion

The dataset itself provides a very surface-level understanding of each animated series as it only shows that series’ attributes and neglects to show the implications that come with the overall dataset. As seen by the visualizations used, breaking down the dataset and focusing on only a couple of attributes at a time can help make certain relationships clearer. For example, the number of shows attribute coupled with the country attribute and the channel attribute reveals that the U.S leads in total shows released. Moreover, out of the top 10 channels by number of shows, the majority of the channels come from the U.S. We’ve learned that the U.S. has released the most amount of animated shows globally. Additionally, the U.S. contains the majority of the top 10 most productive channels. Ostensibly, the most dominant animation technique has been the traditional technique. However, CGI has gained popularity faster over the past 80 years. Could this trend be a possible indicator of the next dominant animation technique? Lastly, in the scatter chart we saw that the majority of shows last 1-18 years and air around 150 episodes.
